We are effectively projecting a vector in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn to Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm by

  • Taking a weighted sum (linear combination) of columns in MMM
  • Case n>mn > mn>m: Projecting into a lower dimensional space
    • An element vi,v∈Rnv_i, v \in \mathbb{R}^nvi​,v∈Rn corresponds to a column vector Mi,Mi∈RmM_i, M_i \in \mathbb{R}^{m}Mi​,Mi​∈Rm in the above matrix-vector multiplication.
    • MiM_iMi​ can be represented as a linear combination of basis vectors in Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm s.t. it's a projection from Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn to Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm corresponding to the iiith basis vector in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn scaled by the weight of viv_ivi​. This weight tells us how important that MiM_iMi​ is in the projection.
      • Projecting a higher dimensional vector (scaled by scalar viv_ivi​) from Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn to Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm results in MiM_iMi​.
        • Yes, this is an actual higher dimensional vector, imagine M=I∈Rn×nM = I \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}M=I∈Rn×n, viv_ivi​ would correspond to the iiith basis vector in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, column vector IiI_iIi​.
      • viv_ivi​ is just a value in the iiith dim of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn.
      • Can be thought of as "compression" from Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn to Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm since n>mn > mn>m
    • MiM_iMi​ can also be linearly dependent on the other columns in MMM.
  • Case n<mn < mn<m: Projecting into a higher dimensional space
    • Similar idea.
    • Now we have less information to project into a higher dimensional space, so we just project using one of the fewer basis vectors of MMM
